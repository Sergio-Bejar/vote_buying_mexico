# Multi-Party Clientelism in Mexico

This repository contains replication materials for a research project on vote buying and clientelistic competition in Mexico, using the CIDE-CSES 2015 National Electoral Study and precinct-level electoral data.

## Papers
### Paper 1: Competitive Clientelism in Mexico's Electoral Precincts
Status: Manuscript drafted, figures and tables complete
Why do competing parties buy votes in the same communities? We document strong positive correlations in precinct-level vote buying rates across six parties (r = 0.34–0.56) and evaluate three competing explanations through a sequential elimination strategy. Supply-side herding on observable electoral signals is ruled out (partial correlations barely change after conditioning). Community-level norms of vote buying acceptability predict convergence. Shared contact infrastructure contributes but does not fully account for the pattern. We argue that the electoral market for votes is structured by community-level demand rather than by broker optimization.

### Paper 2: From Contact to Gift (analysis complete, manuscript pending)
Target:restructuring around phone contact finding
Status: Analysis and figures complete, under evaluation for standalone viability
How do parties convert organizational outreach into clientelistic distribution? Party contact is the strongest within-precinct predictor of gift receipt (r = 0.29), far exceeding demographic characteristics. Phone contact outperforms face-to-face canvassing for PRI. Contact is not a necessary condition for gift receipt. Conversion rates vary sharply by party organizational capacity.
